How to convert SRT to VTT using textedit on Mac

TextEdit comes preinstalled on every Mac and can do basic find-and-replace. To change an SRT to VTT:

  1. Locate your “captions.srt” file in Finder.
  2. Make a copy and rename it “captions.vtt.”
  3. Right-click the .vtt file and open it in TextEdit.
  4. At the very top of the file, add a new first line that reads WEBVTT.
  5. Open the Find bar (Command + F) and search for , (a comma).
  6. Replace all with . (a period) so timing like 00:01:23,500 becomes 00:01:23.500.
  7. Save the file. Your VTT file is ready for your video player.

Best practices tip: Before you rename, duplicate the original .srt. That gives you a fallback if you need to tweak timing or text. This manual method works for short files under a few hundred lines.

How to automate replacement of commas for clearer timing

If you prefer a quick command-line step without adding new software, you can use the Mac Terminal. The built-in sed tool handles find and replace across the file. In plain English, open Terminal, point to your folder and run a single line that:

  • Inserts “WEBVTT” at the top
  • Replaces commas with periods
  • Saves output as a .vtt file

You do not need Homebrew or extra apps. Since Terminal comes standard on Mac, this counts as a no-software solution. Just be mindful that large files could be slower to process than a dedicated tool.

Pitfall to avoid: If your SRT file uses different time formats or includes styling tags, a raw find-and-replace may break the cues. Always skim the first few lines of the resulting VTT file to confirm correct structure.

Best practices for checking your VTT file

Once you have a .vtt file, run through a quick checklist:

  • First line reads exactly WEBVTT with no extra spaces.
  • There is a blank line after the header before your first cue.
  • Time stamps use periods not commas and follow “HH:MM:SS.mmm” format.
  • Your text lines match what you see on screen without odd characters.

If your video player shows gaps or fails to load captions, open the .vtt in your Subtitle editor to spot stray characters or missing cues. This editor also offers preview so you can scrub through your video and see captions in context.
