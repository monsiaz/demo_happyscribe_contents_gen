Why should you validate your VTT after conversion

When you convert srt to vtt it often feels like flipping a switch. Yet the conversion might introduce subtle formatting problems. These can break caption rendering or confuse automated systems that rely on strict VTT rules. Common pain points include:

  • Missing WEBVTT header at the top
  • Timestamps with commas instead of dots (00:01:05,200 vs 00:01:05.200)
  • Overlapping cues that display two lines at once
  • Unescaped characters or broken cues from line breaks
  • Wrong file encoding causing garbled accents or symbols

Failing to catch these can cause your captions to misalign, appear blank, or crash certain players. By validating early you save time on rework and avoid last-minute headaches.

How can you spot common VTT errors quickly

You do not need heavy code or a deep tech background. Here are simple checks you can run in a text editor or with a free online validator:

  • Confirm the first line reads WEBVTT with no extra spaces
  • Scan for commas in timestamps and replace with dots
  • Look for overlaps where the end time of one cue is after the start time of the next
  • Check for unwanted blank lines in the middle of a cue
  • Ensure UTF-8 encoding to handle special characters

Tip: Use a quick search in your text editor for patterns like “,--” or “–>” to catch broken arrows or misformatted timecodes.

What steps can you follow to fix VTT issues by hand

If you only have a handful of errors, a manual approach may be fastest. Here is a clear checklist:

  1. Open the VTT file in a plain text editor (Notepad, TextEdit, VS Code).
  2. Ensure “WEBVTT” is the first line. Delete any characters before it.
  3. Replace commas in all timecodes with dots. You can use search and replace for , to ..
  4. Reorder overlapping cues by splitting them into separate blocks or adjusting start/end times.
  5. Remove extra blank lines within cues to keep speaker lines together.
  6. Save the file in UTF-8 without BOM to avoid encoding errors.

Pitfall: Avoid adding tabs or extra spaces before timecodes. Even a single space before “00:00” can break playback on strict players.
